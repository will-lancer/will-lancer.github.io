About Me

Academics and Interests

I am a Junior in physics and mathematics at Stony Brook University (graduating May 2026). I'll be going to Cambridge in the Fall to start my master's in theoretical physics.

My physics interests mainly lie in quantum field theory, specifically its non-perturbative and on-shell formulations. I think these vectors are useful ones for solving some of the biggest problems in physics today. There is also a sort of experimental duality that comes with these subjects — non-perturbative QFT finds many examples in condensed matter physics, while on-shell QFT has manifold connections with cosmology. More recently, I've been extremely interested in using machine learning to work on the (Millennium) problem of Yang-Mills and the mass gap, and that's what I'm dedicating a lot of my time toward.

I also enjoy pure mathematics, and I think modern physics will require new and more advanced mathematical techniques for its implementation. There are three specific areas of mathematics that are especially interesting to me at the moment: the Geometric Langlands program, the positive Grassmannian, and constructive quantum field theory. Geometric Langlands is mainly an academic interest — I am not sure of its continuing applicability to the physical sciences. The work on the positive Grassmannian and the amplituhedron is also interesting to me, specifically because physicists and mathematicians seem to be working in lockstep here; this seems very unusual to me and that's exciting. Constructive quantum field theory is also interesting to me, but I find analysis painful... so we'll see how much I work on it.

My final big interest is artificial intelligence. I am new to the field, but I find it incredibly fascinating, and I think it's the most important technology of the 21st century. I'm specifically interested in creating better learning algorithms/new architectures for models, as well as using AI for mathematics and theoretical physics — one of the most exciting things about the next ten years will be the (comparative) centuries of theoretical physics and mathematics progress we will make.
