How Much Stuff Can I Learn This Summer?

May 2026

Summer has finally dawned, and it's time to self-study! I have some subjects that I've been wanting to learn deeply, and honestly—I want to get to researching new things! For fun, and for my personal edification, I've decided to meticulously record my learnings (e.g. hours worked, photos of all homeworks, annotated PDFs uploaded, etc.) this summer and document them at this page. Tag along to see how confused I get!

What I'm Trying to Learn

I've decided against making a Huge List That I Will Never Finish™ at the beginning of this experiment and then, well, not finishing it. Rather, I'm just going to do SGD on whatever subject strikes the balance between interesting and useful at that given time. Interspersed in this stochastic process will be a few things that I must cover before Part III: a second pass of QFT, and a full pass of GR at the master's level.

How I Learn

I learn via mind-maps. My meta-goal when learning is to think about things in the simplest and most natural way possible. It is my fervent belief that physics and mathematics are easy, and if something is not easy, you're just not thinking about it in the right way. Here's the general schema for how I enter into a new subject:

  • Pick the right resource. I like a good mix of intuition and precision, and shoot for modern viewpoints of things. I also only read nicely typeset resources—bad typesetting is an indication of laziness to me, especially in the era of AI, and I have no patience for it.
  • Get a general overview of the entire resource. I will read through the entire resource, jot down the main ideas in my mind-map, and start preliminarily organizing things. This will be extremely messy and ugly, and the mind-map won't stay like this. I basically just want to put things in context and get a bird's eye view of the subject. This process usually takes a few hours (2-4), varying with how familiar I am with the subject and how long the resource I'm reading is. This is one of the most important parts of learning to me—I feel blind without it. Nothing makes sense to me unless I can see the natural or obvious context to put it in.
  • Start reading. After getting a general view of things, I just start going through the resource. I think things through from scratch when reading, as I am borderline obsessed with finding the simplest and most natural way to think about things. I construct simple examples of general claims when I read them. I ask a lot of questions and answer them using LLMs, professors, or just by thinking about it for a bit longer. I tend to heavily annotate the PDFs I'm reading because of this. I think of myself as easily confused, in the sense that I cannot tolerate anything less than something being completely obvious, and I stay confused about things until they clarify in this way (or don't...). I occasionally query LLMs during this time if I feel like I'm not understanding something actually important in the text. When reading, I will oftentimes skip more technical points or discussions that I think aren't useful for learning the Big Ideas™ of whatever subject I'm there to learn—this is a feel thing, and I mark down when this occurs so I can come back to these discussions one day. I also will tend to skip more detailed worked examples, as I think it's usually better to just derive these things yourself. Note that I will be updating and re-organizing my mind-map while I'm reading, but it's not super frequently that I do this. Most of the time I have the notes open and I'm just reading them.
  • Do problems. After reading a chapter/watching a few lectures of some resource, I will find some exercises so I can stretch my legs and think about what I just learned. I view problems more as ways to investigate the subject at hand rather than these big chunky things that you need to be clever to do. Again, if a problem is hard, I think you probably just haven't found a good way of thinking about it yet. Physics should be easy. Nature is smart, and it's your job to try to figure out the right ways of thinking about things so you're as smart as Her!
  • Re-read what I just learned and answer questions. After doing some problems so things are more stuck in my brain, I go through my annotated notes and resolve loose ends. This means I think through the ten millions questions I left for myself, read the more techincal sections I skipped, and do any derivations that I marked for myself to do. LLMs are very useful for answering doubts here.
  • Rinse and repeat!

What I'll be recording

For posterity, I'll be recording the following statistics:

  • Total hours studied per subject. This will be recorded with a stop watch and is strict: no dilly-dallying allowed on the clock and all work must be recorded. I will not lie about this, as I selfishly do not want to poison this extremely useful data.
  • Photos of all homeworks done.
  • Upload of the annotated PDF I'm reading (if applicable).
  • Upload of completed mind-map at the end of the course.
  • List of unanswered questions at the end of the course.

What Counts as Success

This is going to be judged on vibes, as I'm not trying to win any accolades by doing this. I know when I know something at this point. The basic metric of success in any course is problem-solving ability, so if I solve all of the problem sheets for the course, I think I'm in decent shape.
